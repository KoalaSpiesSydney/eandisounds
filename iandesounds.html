HINT — The Varying Sounds of A

Lesson: Listening and speaking are connected. To make the sound you need to notice how your mouth, lips, tongue, and jaw move. Click the short videos below to hear each sound clearly.

/æ/— long “a” sound (cat, can,hand,jam)

/æ/ is the short “a” sound you hear in cat, map, and black. Your mouth opens a bit, your tongue sits low and forward, and the sound is quick and relaxed.

/eɪ/ — long and open “a” sound (Kate, cane, cake)

/eɪ/ is the long “ay” sound you hear in cake, rain, and name. Your mouth starts in a mid‑open e position, then your tongue moves slightly higher as the sound glides forward.

/aː/ or /ɑː/ — “ah” sound (dance)

The /aː/ vowel is made with the tongue low and slightly forward, the jaw open, and the lips relaxed. The vocal folds vibrate smoothly to create a long, open, voiced sound.

The /aː/ vowel is made with the tongue low and but in the middle a little back, the jaw less open, and the lips relaxed. The vocal folds vibrate smoothly to create a long, open, voiced sound.

/ɔː/ — “all” sound (call)

Your mouth opens wide, your tongue sits low in the front of your mouth, and your voice vibrates the whole time.

/ɛ/ — “e” sound (many)

/ɛ/ is the short “e” sound, like in bed, pen, or many. Your mouth stays half‑open, your tongue is in the middle‑front of your mouth, and your lips stay relaxed

/ɒ/ — “o” sound (want)

/ɒ/ is the short “o” sound you hear in hot, not, want, and squat in Australian English. Your mouth opens a little, your lips relax (not rounded), and your tongue sits low and slightly back.
